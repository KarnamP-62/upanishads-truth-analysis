WHAT?

The Upanishads are late Vedic Sanskrit texts that form the concluding, wisdom-centered portion of the Vedas. Composed roughly between 700–300 BCE, they explore themes of meditation, philosophy, consciousness, and the nature of reality. A central teaching is that the individual soul, Atman, is identical to the ultimate universal reality, Brahman. The texts also engage with concepts such as karma, reincarnation, and enlightenment, emphasizing knowledge and self-realization over ritual practices. Traditionally, 108 Upanishads are recognized and associated with the four Vedas: Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, and Atharvaveda.

HOW?

This project applies natural language processing (NLP) to examine the emotional and philosophical contexts surrounding the concept of "truth" across the Upanishadic texts. The analysis focuses on several key terms associated with the idea of truth: Truth, Satya (the Sanskrit term for truth), Sat (meaning true, real, abiding, or being), and Tattva, a foundational Sanskrit philosophical concept meaning "thatness," "principle," "reality," or "truth." These words capture different dimensions of truth as expressed within the Upanishadic texts.

To support this analysis, the TF-IDF method is used to identify the frequency and importance of key terms. Sentence-BERT (SBERT) is applied to understand semantic and philosophical context through sentence embeddings, while semantic analysis techniques are used to interpret the emotional tone of sentences containing references to truth. The source text for this analysis is the compilation of 108 Upanishads, available here: 108 Upanishads PDF

WHY?

The visualizations explore how truth is expressed within the Upanishads, highlighting both its emotional resonance and its philosophical dimensions. By combining computational methods with ancient wisdom texts, this project offers a unique lens through which to examine one of humanity's most enduring concepts.

Do you know? The Indian national motto, "Satyameva Jayate" meaning "Truth alone triumphs", is derived from the Mundaka Upanishad, a text associated with the Atharvaveda.

Emotional Landscape of Truth

Truth in the Upanishads is not only an intellectual concept but also evokes profound emotional responses. This visualization organizes those responses into five thematic patterns, each associated with a set of emotions. Each plant represents a theme, while the branches of the plant represent different emotions within that theme. The petals on each branch indicate occurrences of sentences containing references to truth. The colors correspond to the four Vedas. Within each branch, petals are arranged vertically according to emotional intensity, with sentences having higher emotion scores placed toward the top of the plant and those with lower emotional scores positioned toward the bottom.

  • Connection - Unity, devotion, reverence
  • Spiritual - Transcendence, liberation, bliss
  • Contemplative - Peace, serenity, detachment
  • Knowledge - Understanding, wisdom, enlightenment, wonder, awe
  • Challenge - Fear, confusion, longing, determination

Overview

Among all the emotions analyzed, wisdom and unity show the strongest associations with truth in the Upanishads.

Lotus Pond of Philosophical Truth

Each lotus flower represents a distinct philosophical context in which the concept of truth appears within the Upanishadic texts. The four petals of the lotus correspond to the four Vedas. Lines within each petal indicate individual occurrences of truth within sentences from the texts. These lines are arranged from left to right according to their contextual relevance, progressing from lower context scores to higher context scores. The lotus, traditionally a symbol of enlightenment and spiritual awakening, reflects the unfolding of philosophical insight surrounding the concept of truth.

  • Liberation & Freedom - Moksha and release from bondage
  • Cosmic Elements - Truth in nature and universe
  • Knowledge & Wisdom - Jnana and understanding
  • Sacred Utterances - Mantras and holy words
  • Self-Realization - Atman and inner truth
  • Teacher-Student - Transmission of wisdom
  • Brahman Identity - Ultimate reality
  • Ethical Foundation - Truth as moral principle

Overview

Among all philosophical contexts, Cosmic Elements appears most frequently across the Upanishads, followed by Knowledge & Wisdom and Brahman Identity.
